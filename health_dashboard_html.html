Key Research Findings

1Ô∏è‚É£ Geographic Disparities in Health Access

Finding: Annual checkup rates vary by 15.6 percentage points across Massachusetts counties (67.9% to 83.5%).

Analysis: Urban counties like Suffolk (Boston) and Middlesex show 8-10% higher preventive care utilization compared to rural counties like Berkshire and Franklin.

Statistical Significance: Correlation between population size and checkup rates: r = 0.67 (p < 0.05)

Impact: This gap represents approximately 11,000 fewer people in low-performing counties receiving recommended annual preventive care.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Population Size Effect on Healthcare Access

Finding: Larger counties demonstrate 8% higher average screening rates across all preventive measures.

Regression Results: For every 100,000 increase in population, checkup rates increase by approximately 1.2 percentage points (Œ≤ = 0.012, p = 0.03).

Interpretation: Economies of scale, infrastructure density, and provider availability create systematic advantages for urban populations.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Healthcare Workforce Aging Crisis

Finding: 28.4% of Massachusetts healthcare workforce is age 60 or older, representing 89,582 workers approaching retirement.

Critical Area: Physicians show highest risk at 35.2% age 60+ (20,026 physicians).

Timeline: Assuming retirement age 65-70, Massachusetts could lose 15-20% of its physician workforce within 5-10 years without pipeline development.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Workforce Diversity Gap

Finding: Hispanic workers represent 8.5% of healthcare workforce despite being 12% of Massachusetts population (3.5 percentage point gap).

Context: Research shows diverse workforces improve cultural competency and health outcomes for minority populations.

Opportunity: Targeted pipeline programs could both address retirement wave and improve workforce diversity simultaneously.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Preventive Care Measure Correlations

Finding: Strong positive correlations exist between different preventive care measures (r = 0.72-0.85).

Interpretation: Counties that excel in one preventive measure tend to excel across the board, suggesting systemic factors (access, insurance, health literacy) drive overall performance rather than measure-specific issues.

Policy Implication: Interventions should address root causes of access barriers rather than targeting individual screening types.
